A design case study

Making hard-to-adopt
products desirable.

A study by Verônica Vanti on redesigning fall prevention — from a symbol of decline into an object older adults actually want to own.

The thesis

Adoption is a design problem, not a technology problem.

Most fall prevention tools already work. They track gait. They flag risk. They prompt exercise. And yet the people who need them most — older adults living independently — quietly refuse to use them.

The failure is not clinical. It is aesthetic, emotional, and cultural. Medical objects in the home announce decline. A smartphone dependency excludes anyone who never adopted the habit. A product designed for older adults, instead of with them, becomes a burden — no matter how good the sensor.

Neevel started from a different premise: that the object in the room has to feel like something a grandchild would gift. Not clinical. Not apologetic. Quiet, warm, and welcome.

Prevention should feel less like a device and more like care, dignity, and routine.

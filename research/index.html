Research

Sparse-sensor telemetry
for low-cost gait screening.

The open research question behind Neevel: how much clinically meaningful gait signal can be preserved when sensing is reduced to a cheap three-FSR insole?

The question

Rich sensing is expensive. Sparse sensing is unknown.

Research-grade instrumented insoles — sixteen or more pressure points, IMUs, and wired data acquisition — produce the cleanest gait signals available outside a motion capture lab. They also cost hundreds to thousands of dollars per pair and live almost entirely inside research studies.

Neevel's prototype uses three force-sensitive resistors per foot. Roughly an order of magnitude less data than the clinical reference. The question is not whether three sensors match sixteen — they do not. The question is how much of the clinically meaningful signal — cadence, stride timing, asymmetry proxies, sit-to-stand rep detection — survives the reduction.

If enough signal survives, low-cost wearable screening becomes a different category of thing: daily, ambient, and scaled.

Approach

Public datasets, then hardware.

The research track begins with openly available gait datasets — PhysioNet's Gait in Aging and Disease, the Smart-Insole Dataset, GaitRec, fall datasets like SisFall and UP-Fall — where rich sensor input is already paired with clinical labels.

From there: downsample. Simulate the sparse three-FSR input that Neevel's hardware produces. Measure what survives. Build the feature pipeline — stride timing, cadence, asymmetry proxies, sit-to-stand rep counting — against established baselines. Then bring it back to physical hardware for validation.

Stage 01
Baseline reproduction.
Reproduce existing baselines on public gait and fall datasets. If reproduction is not possible, the technical track is not pursued.
Stage 02
Feature pipeline.
Stride timing, cadence, asymmetry proxies, and sit-to-stand rep counting — extracted from pressure signal alone.
Stage 03
Sparse simulation.
Train teacher models on rich pressure data. Downsample to three-point input. Quantify what signal survives the reduction.
Stage 04
Hardware validation.
Bring the pipeline back to the Neevel prototype. Collect small internal dataset with video-anchored labels.

A note on claims

What Neevel is, and is not, claiming.

Neevel is a wellness product and an active research platform. It is not a diagnostic device.

Balance exercise has well-established effects on fall rates in older adults — the CDC STEADI framework cites reductions of 24 to 42 percent across three hours per week of structured practice. Neevel aims to make that practice easier to sustain, inside the home, without a smartphone.

What Neevel does not claim, and will not claim without validation: fall prediction at the individual level, diagnosis of fall risk, clinical equivalence with force plates, or outcomes that require prospective clinical trial evidence. The research track exists to stay honest about that boundary.

For collaborators

Research partners welcome.

Neevel's research track is open to collaboration with gait labs, geriatrics programs, physical-therapy clinics, and graduate students working on adjacent problems.
